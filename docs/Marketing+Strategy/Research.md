Recent text-to-video diffusion models have pushed video lengths to roughly the ten-second level at high qualityar5iv.labs.arxiv.org. Autoregressive “Streaming” Generation: New methods generate long videos by dividing output into manageable chunks while preserving continuity. FIFO-Diffusion and Rolling Diffusion introduced a queue-based generation: frames are generated sequentially in a first-in-first-out manner, allowing an infinite-length video in principle by continuously appending new noise frames after older ones are finalizedar5iv.labs.arxiv.org. More recently, StreamingT2V (CVPR 2025) demonstrated an advanced autoregressive pipeline that turns base text-to-video models into long video generatorsgithub.com. For example, StreamingT2V’s implementation with Stable Diffusion (termed StreamingSVD) can produce ~8-second (200-frame) continuous clips and can be extended further without fine-tuninggithub.com. By chaining such chunks, another version using ModelScope’s video model achieved up to 2 minutes of fluid, non-repeating video with maintained motion and qualitygithub.com. These streaming approaches ensure temporal consistency by carrying information from previous frames into the generation of new frames, avoiding the “reset” effect of generating independent clips.
Temporal Attention and Consistency Enhancements: Even with longer generation, flicker and scene jumps can occur if the model focuses too narrowly on each framear5iv.labs.arxiv.org. To address this, researchers have proposed plug-in algorithms to refine the diffusion process for smoothness. One example is TiARA (Time-frequency Attention Reweighting), a training-free method that adjusts the temporal self-attention in diffusion modelsar5iv.labs.arxiv.orgar5iv.labs.arxiv.org. TiARA analyzes the frequency of motions and de-emphasizes frame-specific attention (especially for slow-changing content) so that each frame uses information from its neighbors, greatly reducing abrupt changes in objects or backgroundsar5iv.labs.arxiv.orgar5iv.labs.arxiv.org. This has been shown to significantly improve object and background consistency across long sequences when applied on top of existing video diffusion pipelinesar5iv.labs.arxiv.orgar5iv.labs.arxiv.org. For multi-part prompts, an associated technique called PromptBlend aligns and interpolates textual embeddings to produce smooth scene transitionswhen the prompt changes mid-videoar5iv.labs.arxiv.orgar5iv.labs.arxiv.org. These techniques together tackle the common 2–5 minute video issues of identity drift (subjects changing appearance) and jittery scene cuts, without requiring retraining the model.

Extending beyond ~5 minutes enters uncharted territory for today’s video generators. No single model readily generates 20+ minutes of unique, coherent footage. However, recent research suggests how to reach 10–30 minute scales by structuring the task (multi-scene planning, memory retrieval) and combining tools creatively. The key idea is to break the long video into manageable segments while ensuring global consistency (characters, story, environment) across those segments.
Multi-Scene Story Planning with LLMs: One major advancement, aimed at longer narrative videos, is using large language models to plan and maintain consistency across scenes. This plan includes a sequence of scene descriptions, a list of entities (characters/objects) and backgrounds per scene, and importantly consistency groupings indicating which entities/backgrounds persist across which scenesvideodirectorgpt.github.iovideodirectorgpt.github.io. Using this high-level plan, their video generator (Layout2Vid) can enforce that the same character appears with the same appearance in all relevant scenes by using shared embeddings or “layout” guidancevideodirectorgpt.github.iovideodirectorgpt.github.io. Essentially, VideoDirectorGPT decouples a long video into scenes (e.g. imagine a 15-minute short film with distinct scenes) but ensures continuity by carrying over the planned elements. Experiments have shown this yields visually consistent multi-scene videos and better control over layouts and movements, without requiring video-level training data for everythingvideodirectorgpt.github.io. Memory Retrieval for Long-Term Consistency: If the aim is one continuous scene (e.g. a continuous 20-minute gameplay or a single-take camera shot), a big problem is long-term memory. Recent work points out that state-of-the-art models can technically generate many frames in a sequence, but they forget earlier content – for example, if a camera goes in a circle, when it returns to a previously seen area, the model generates something completely new instead of the original scenearxiv.org. To overcome this, researchers propose treating all past frames as a “memory” that the model can reference. The Context-as-Memory design (2025) suggests storing historical frames and retrieving only the relevant ones to feed into the model when generating a new framearxiv.orgarxiv.org. By pulling in earlier frames that overlap in context (e.g. same camera viewpoint or location) as guidance, the model can preserve scene details over very long timesarxiv.orgarxiv.org. In practice, this might be implemented via a retrieval system: e.g., if frame 1000 has a view of a place seen at frame 100, the system finds frame 100’s image to remind the generator of what that place looked like, preventing divergence. While this is cutting-edge research, the concept can be applied in a vibe-coded solution by using control mechanisms. For instance, one could periodically re-introduce earlier key frames as conditioning images or via ControlNet (using depth or pose from a past frame to constrain the new frame). This ensures global consistency (the same environment or character state) even over tens of minutes. It’s an extra layer on top of the streaming generation: essentially a manual “memory” loop that a system integrator could script, retrieving and feeding back frames at intervals.
Scaling and Fine-Tuning Tools: For very long videos, it might be necessary to utilize fine-tuning of models on specific content to avoid degeneration over time.
